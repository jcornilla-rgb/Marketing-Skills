# Marketing Brainstorm: Goals Into Actionable Strategy

## Purpose

Turn rough marketing goals into validated, executable strategies through structured back-and-forth dialogue. The goal is to surface the real business problem behind the marketing ask, map the current state of the funnel, identify the highest-leverage opportunities, and land on a concrete plan the user feels confident executing.

This isn't about generating a list of tactics. It's about figuring out the right strategy first, then selecting the tactics that serve it.

## Why This Matters

Most wasted marketing spend comes from skipping the strategy step. Someone decides "we need to run Facebook ads" or "we should start a newsletter" without first asking whether that's the right move given their funnel, audience, unit economics, and competitive landscape. Even five minutes of structured questioning prevents months of budget burn on the wrong channel.

## The Process

### Step 1: Understand the Business Context

Before asking any questions, orient yourself:

- If the user has shared data, documents, or context earlier in the conversation, review it
- If you have memory of the user's company, role, industry, or past marketing work, factor that in naturally
- If the request references a specific product, brand, or campaign, acknowledge what you already know

Then summarize your understanding in 2-3 sentences and ask whether you've got it right. This prevents the user from re-explaining things you should already know.

### Step 2: Assess Scope

Evaluate the size of what the user is asking:

- **If the request spans multiple independent marketing workstreams** (e.g., "we need a full rebrand plus a new paid strategy plus a content engine plus an email nurture system"), flag this immediately. Help the user prioritize and sequence, then brainstorm the highest-priority workstream first.
- **If the request is a single focused goal** (e.g., "increase demo bookings" or "figure out our Q3 ad budget"), proceed to clarifying questions.

Each workstream gets its own brainstorm-to-strategy cycle.

### Step 3: Diagnose the Funnel

This is the most important step and the one most people skip. Before recommending any tactic, you need to understand where the funnel is leaking.

Ask questions **one at a time**, and let the user's answers shape what you ask next.

**Core diagnostic questions (adapt to context, don't ask all of these mechanically):**

**The Business:**
- What do you sell, and to whom? (Product/service, price point, customer profile)
- What's your current revenue or volume baseline? (So recommendations can be proportional)
- What's your business model? (One-time purchase, subscription, high-ticket B2B, etc.)

**The Funnel:**
- Where are your customers coming from right now? (Channels, referral sources, organic vs. paid mix)
- What does your conversion path look like? (Ad to landing page to sign-up to purchase, or something else?)
- Where are people dropping off? (If the user doesn't know, that's a data gap worth flagging)
- What's your current CAC (customer acquisition cost) and LTV (lifetime value)?

**The Goal:**
- What specific number are you trying to move? (Revenue, leads, sign-ups, demo bookings, ROAS, etc.)
- By when? (Timeline changes everything about what's realistic)
- What's your budget range? (Even a rough number helps: $500/mo vs. $50K/mo are different universes)

**The Landscape:**
- Who are your top 2-3 competitors and how are they acquiring customers?
- What have you tried before? What worked, what didn't, and why?
- Are there seasonal patterns, industry events, or external timing factors to account for?

**The Constraints:**
- What's your team capacity? (Solo founder vs. marketing team of 10 changes the plan)
- Are there brand, regulatory, or compliance constraints? (Especially relevant for medical, finance, legal)
- What tools and platforms are you already using?

Typically 5-8 questions is enough. Don't over-interview. If the picture is clear after 4 questions, move on. If the user doesn't have data for something (like CAC or conversion rates), note it as a gap and work with what's available.

### Step 4: Identify the Strategic Lever

Based on the diagnostic, identify which lever will have the highest impact. The user asked a tactical question ("where should I spend my budget?"), but your job is to reframe it as a strategic one.

**Common strategic frames:**

- **Awareness problem:** The audience doesn't know you exist. Priority: top-of-funnel visibility (content, ads, PR, partnerships).
- **Traffic-to-lead problem:** You're getting eyeballs but not conversions. Priority: offer optimization, landing page CRO, lead magnets, retargeting.
- **Lead-to-customer problem:** You're generating leads but they're not buying. Priority: nurture sequences, sales enablement, objection handling, pricing/positioning.
- **Retention problem:** You're acquiring customers but they're churning. Priority: onboarding, engagement loops, loyalty programs, reactivation campaigns.
- **Unit economics problem:** You're growing but not profitably. Priority: CAC reduction, LTV expansion, channel mix rebalancing.
- **Positioning problem:** You're competing on price because the market doesn't understand your differentiation. Priority: messaging, brand narrative, competitive positioning.

Name the lever explicitly. "Based on what you've described, this looks like a traffic-to-lead conversion problem more than an awareness problem. Here's why..."

### Step 5: Propose 2-3 Approaches

Present genuinely different strategic directions, not three variations of the same idea.

**Format for each approach:**

- A short label (e.g., "The Paid Blitz," "The Organic Flywheel," "The Partnership Play")
- 2-4 sentences on how it works
- Estimated timeline to results (important for setting expectations)
- Budget implications (ballpark, not line items)
- Key trade-off: what you gain vs. what you give up
- Your honest recommendation and reasoning

Lead with your recommended option. The user wants your informed opinion, not a neutral menu.

### Step 6: Build the Strategy

Once the user picks a direction, flesh it out. Scale the depth to the complexity:

- **Simple decisions** (e.g., "should I run Google Ads or Meta Ads for this product?"): A direct recommendation with reasoning, expected CPM/CPC ranges, audience targeting guidance, and a testing framework. 100-200 words.
- **Medium projects** (e.g., "plan our Q3 content strategy"): Channel selection, content themes, publishing cadence, distribution plan, KPIs. 300-600 words.
- **Complex strategies** (e.g., "build our go-to-market for a new product launch"): Full phased plan with pre-launch, launch, and post-launch activities, channel mix, budget allocation, team responsibilities, measurement framework. 600-1500 words.

**Present in sections, not all at once.** After each section, ask: "Does this track so far?" Catch misalignment early.

**Sections to cover (pick what's relevant):**

- Strategic objective and success metrics
- Target audience and segmentation
- Channel strategy and rationale
- Budget allocation with reasoning (percentages or ranges, not false precision)
- Content and messaging direction
- Timeline and phasing
- Measurement plan: what to track, when to evaluate, when to pivot
- Quick wins: what can start this week vs. what needs more setup
- Open questions: things that need answers but shouldn't block the plan

### Step 7: Document the Strategy

Once the user approves the strategy, write it up as a clean standalone document:

- If the user wants a file, create a markdown document with the full strategy
- If the conversation is the record, provide a clean summary the user can copy
- If the user has a preferred format, follow their lead

The strategy doc should be self-contained. Someone who wasn't in the brainstorming conversation should be able to read it and understand the plan, the reasoning behind it, and what to do first.

### Step 8: Transition to Execution

Ask the user what they want to do next:

- Break it into a week-by-week action plan
- Draft specific assets (ad copy, email sequences, landing page wireframes, content briefs)
- Build a measurement dashboard or tracking setup
- Create a budget spreadsheet with line items
- Share it with stakeholders for feedback
- Start executing the first quick win immediately

Don't assume the next step. Let the user drive.

## Key Principles

**Diagnose before prescribing.** The user asked "where should I put my budget?" but the answer depends on where the funnel is leaking. Find the leak first.

**One question at a time.** Multiple questions produce shallow answers. Take turns.

**YAGNI applies to marketing too.** Don't recommend a 7-channel omnichannel strategy when the user is a solo founder with $2K/month. Recommend the one or two things that will actually move the needle given their constraints.

**Name the trade-offs.** "Paid gives you speed but costs cash. Organic gives you compounding returns but takes 3-6 months to show up." Let the user make informed choices.

**Quantify where possible.** "You should try email marketing" is weak. "Based on your 5,000-person list and a 2% conversion rate at your $80 AOV, a well-executed 4-email launch sequence could generate roughly $8K in revenue" is useful.

**Separate strategy from tactics.** Strategy is which game to play. Tactics are the moves within that game. Get the strategy right first. Don't let "should I use Reels or TikTok?" derail the conversation before you've established whether short-form video is even the right play.

**Flag data gaps honestly.** If the user doesn't know their CAC or can't tell you where customers drop off, say so. Recommend lightweight ways to get that data (even rough estimates) rather than building a strategy on assumptions.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Jumping to tactics without diagnosis.** "You should run Google Ads" before understanding the funnel is malpractice.
- **Recommending everything.** A strategy that says "do SEO and paid ads and email and social and PR and partnerships" is not a strategy. It's a wishlist. Prioritize ruthlessly.
- **Ignoring unit economics.** A campaign that generates leads at $50 each is great if LTV is $500. It's a disaster if LTV is $60. Always sanity-check the math.
- **Treating all channels equally.** Not every channel works for every business. A B2B SaaS company and a local bakery have fundamentally different acquisition playbooks. Match the channel to the business model.
- **Over-planning without quick wins.** Every strategy should include something the user can start doing this week, not just a 90-day roadmap.
- **Presenting false precision.** "Your ROAS will be 4.2x" is a guess. "Based on industry benchmarks for your category, ROAS of 3-5x is realistic if the creative performs" is honest.
- **Skipping the "what have you tried?" question.** The user may have already burned $10K on the exact thing you're about to recommend. Ask first.
